Threats to Sea Turtles

Illegal harvesting, habitat encroachment, and pollution are only some of the things sea turtles must fight against to stay alive. Researchers at FWRI are studying these threats and finding ways to help the population survive.


Unfortunately, sea turtle populations around the world are on a sharp decline due to natural predators and severe human interference. One of the biggest problems in Sri Lanka are human poachers, who mishandle the eggs to sell to turtle sanctuaries or to locals as a source of food or medicine.

Marine turtles in Sri Lanka face both natural and human threats. Natural threats include predators on the beach and natural disasters. Human threats include direct killing and harvesting of female turtles, destruction of nesting and foraging grounds, and increased human activities on beaches. Pollution and obstacles on beaches, as well as hatchlings being exploited for the pet trade, affect the hatchling stage and their descent into the ocean. It is important to protect sea turtles due to their contribution to the sustenance of oceanic eco systems and the health of world oceans. Green turtles consume seagrass, hawksbill turtles consume toxic sponges, leatherbacks and loggerheads regulate the population of jellyfish and crustaceans, and provide habitat for barnacles, algae, and epibionts. All species of sea turtles are considered "endangered" in today's world, so it is essential to conserve and ensure the safety of all species.

Turtle Conservation Project - Sri Lanka


Marine turtles are protected under Sri Lanka government legislation. The TCP is an independent Sri Lankan NGO which was established in 1993 and in March 1995 registered under the Central Environmental authority (CEA). The TCP operates according to an approved constitution and is currently coordinated by a committee of seven residents of Sri Lanka. TCP aims to devise and facilitate the implementation of sustainable marine turtle conservation strategies through education, research and community participation and assisting government departments, academic institutions and other NGOs
